Sources & methodology

Approach

This atlas traces 18 critical minerals and rare earth elements across three stages of their supply chains — mining (raw extraction), processing (concentration and chemical conversion), and refining (production of the final purified form used in chips, magnets, or batteries) — for materials that the AI industry materially depends on, whether for the silicon itself, the data centers that house it, or the power and storage systems that feed it.

Country shares are approximate global percentages drawn from public 2024–2025 production data. They should be read as orders of magnitude, not precise figures: sources disagree by a few percentage points on individual countries, undocumented production (particularly Myanmar rare earths and artisanal mining in the DRC) does not always appear in official statistics, and for some commodities the "processing" and "refining" stages are functionally fused within a single industrial complex.

Caveats worth knowing

  • Many of these minerals are dual-use. Silicon production is dominated by solar, copper by general electrification, lithium by EVs. Shares reflect total global production rather than the AI-specific slice.
  • Rare-earth aggregates, where cited, refer to the four magnet REEs — neodymium, praseodymium, dysprosium, and terbium — the IEA's standard reporting category.
  • For silicon, "refining" here means semiconductor wafer fabrication (TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix). Polysilicon production sits under "processing."
  • Refining shares for cobalt, nickel, and lithium reflect battery-grade chemicals (sulfates and hydroxides), not refined metal.
  • Hafnium figures are estimated — the commercial market is small and reporting is sparse.
  • Selection of which minerals qualify as "AI-critical" is editorial. The threshold was "plays a non-trivial role in building, powering, cooling, storing, or interconnecting AI compute."

Primary sources

  • U.S. Geological Survey — Mineral Commodity Summaries 2025 Annual reference for global production, reserves, and trade flows of critical mineral commodities.
  • IEA — Global Critical Minerals Outlook 2025 Supply concentration analysis and export-control tracking, including the December 2024 Chinese restrictions on gallium, germanium, and antimony.
  • IEA — Rare Earth Elements (2025) Mining, refining, and magnet-manufacturing breakdowns for the magnet REE group; tracks the April and October 2025 Chinese heavy-REE export controls.
  • CSIS — Developing Rare Earth Processing Hubs (July 2025) Coverage of non-Chinese refining buildout: Lynas Malaysia, Iluka Eneabba, Arafura Nolans, Solvay La Rochelle.
  • Cobalt Institute — Cobalt Market Report 2024 (May 2025) DRC and Indonesian mining splits and Chinese refining concentration; HPAL ramp-up data.
  • Bernreuter Research — Polysilicon Market Outlook 2029 2024 polysilicon production rankings; cited the 93.5% Chinese share and Wacker's standing as the lone non-Chinese top-10 producer.
  • Visual Capitalist — Elements Aggregated supply-chain visualizations cross-referencing USGS, IEA, World Economic Forum, and Wood Mackenzie figures.
  • Mining Technology Operator-level reporting on individual mines: Tenke Fungurume, Kisanfu, Mt Weld, Mountain Pass, Las Bambas.
